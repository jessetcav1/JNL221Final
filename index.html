Which NFL position got the most concussions during the 2024 NFL season?

By: Jesse Cavaliero

Despite ongoing efforts to reduce head injuries in professional football, concussions remain a serious issue across all NFL positions. I have divided the positions into eight parts for each major positon group as follows: Defensive Backs (DB), Defensive Lineman (DL), Linebackers (LB), Offensive Lineman (OL), Quarterbacks (QB), Running Backs (RB), Tight End (TE), Wide Reciever (RB). An analysis of the weekly injury reports from the 2024 season reveals that defensive backs (DBs) suffered the highest number of concussions, with 37 recorded incidents, leading all positions. The data underscores the physical toll on players in high-speed, high-impact roles and highlights the persistent challenge of protecting athletes in America’s most popular sport.

Defensive backs are responsible for pass coverage, tackling, and defending against deep throws and short-yardage plays. Their role often puts them in direct contact with offensive players at full speed, whether diving to break up a pass or tackling a wide receiver in space. These interactions routinely involve rapid changes in direction and momentum, increasing the likelihood of head collisions. The 37 reported concussions among DBs represent over 25% of the league-wide total. A defensive back's average height and weight are 5 feet 11 inches and 195 pounds. With this in mind, defensive backs are much smaller than the average NFL player, with the average NFL player standing at 6 feet 2 and 216 pounds.

Following closely behind, offensive linemen accounted for 33 concussions in 2024. While linemen are not typically associated with high-speed collisions, their constant engagement in short-range, repetitive contact in the trenches makes them vulnerable to sub-concussive impacts and helmet-to-helmet blows. Each play begins with a hit, accumulating throughout a game or season, increasing concussion risk. The high number among OL suggests that even low-velocity impacts, when sustained regularly, can result in severe brain injuries.

Wide receivers experienced 26 concussions, the third-highest total. Their routes often end with airborne catches or abrupt stops, making them prime targets for hits from multiple directions. When WRs are hit mid-air or just after catching the ball, the sudden impact against the turf or from a defensive player can lead to head trauma. These injuries often generate the most public attention due to their dramatic nature and the visibility of WRs in highlight reels.

Linebackers, who play a hybrid role of pass coverage and run defense, recorded 18 concussions. Like DBs, LBs are in frequent contact with fast-moving offensive players, but they also endure repeated collisions at the line of scrimmage, particularly when filling gaps to stop the run. Their dual-threat responsibilities expose them to a range of high-impact scenarios.

Other positions with notable concussion totals include running backs with 14, tight ends with 9, defensive linemen with 6, and quarterbacks with 4.. The relatively low number for quarterbacks reflects successful rule changes and protective measures to shield them from late or unnecessary hits.

In total, the NFL recorded 147 concussions during the 2024 season. These numbers are a reminder that while concussion protocols and spotters have improved player safety, the game remains inherently violent. The distribution of concussions across positions also shows that no role is immune, but some are at higher risk due to the nature of their responsibilities. It's important to remember that these concussions not only affect the current season but can also have long-term implications for players' careers and quality of life. This understanding should foster empathy towards the players and their sacrifices for the game.

Football is not the only sport with major concussion problems. The WWE's Coordinator for US Business Development and Global Partnerships, Alex Cohn, has had trouble marketing fighters who have suffered head trauma in the past. Cohn's experience with the WWE fighters serves as a stark reminder of the serious consequences of head injuries in sports. Cohn said, “Unfortunately, injuries—including concussions—are an inherent part of sports, and MMA and wrestling are no exception. As part of the sales and marketing team, many of our partnerships are built around showcasing our incredibly talented athletes and superstars. When an injury occurs, especially something as serious as a concussion, it limits our ability to feature them on the biggest stages and can impact key marketing opportunities.” The commercial aspects of the sport are heavily impacted due to head injuries, among other health concerns, across every sport.

In recent years, the league has taken various steps to curb head injuries, including updated helmet technology, stricter roughing-the-passer penalties, and revised tackling techniques that discourage helmet-first contact. The potential for further improvements, such as shortening the season, reducing full-contact practices, and evolving rules to de-emphasize high-impact plays, offers hope for the game's future. These potential changes show that the NFL is committed to making the game safer for its players.

Ultimately, the data from 2024 shows that defensive players, particularly those in the secondary, bear the most significant burden when it comes to head injuries. With nearly 40 concussions recorded among defensive backs alone, the NFL faces renewed pressure to examine how the structure of the game and its expectations for defensive performance contribute to brain trauma.

As the 2025 season approaches, the unwavering commitment of medical experts, coaches, and league officials to evaluate these statistics and build the game's future is a source of reassurance. Their dedication is a testament to the ongoing efforts to improve player safety. In the meantime, defensive backs will continue to play one of the most dangerous roles on the field, tasked with stopping the pass in an environment where the next hit could have lasting consequences.
